Who was Horus?



Horus was a main god in the early mythology of ancient Egypt. He represented a sky god/king god, as well as a war god, a world or light god and he was the protector of children. He was usually depicted as a falcon.

The oldest being of the god Horus was that of a sky god, with the right eye being the so-called sun eye and the left eye the moon eye. Various myths surround both eyes. In his significance as the emblem of a victorious people, Horus became a war god and war-bringing leader, which gave rise to the belief that the king (pharaoh) was his earthly embodiment.

Since that time, the kings of Egypt bore the falcon god Horus in their royal title. In later times, the Egyptians merged the sun Horus with the god of the same name from the Osiris cult to create one god Horus. However, this fusion process (syncretism) led to different results in different cult centers, so that there were ultimately fifteen different Horus gods.

Accordingly Horus is the son of Osiris and Isis. He is locked in battle with Set, his uncle, who killed Horus' father, Osiris. He tries to avenge the murder of his father and becomes pharaoh. Therefore, every living pharaoh is equated with Horus: The respective pharaoh is Horus, i.e. a god.

According to myth, Osiris was the first pharaoh and the one who unified Egypt. He was directly related to the gods, his bloodline stretching back to the creator god Atum. Osiris ruled Egypt together with the goddess Isis, his wife, and their rule ensured that balance and justice (Ma'at) were maintained.

In his left hand, Horus holds an "ankh" (a symbol similar to a cross), the hieroglyph for the word "life" in the ancient Egyptian language.

On the left side you see the symbol of "The Eye of Horus".

In modern Egypt, too, a connection to Horus is very popular, probably due to the Muslim Arabs' appreciation of hunting falcons. The national airline Egypt Air has Horus as its signet and logo, and the business class was formerly called Horus Class. Ships travelling on the Nile have the Eye of Horus painted on both sides of the bow to ward off bad luck.

    There is no mention of the god Horus ever dying in Egyptian mythology; as a god he is immortal and therefore without end. However, practically speaking, it could be said that Horus died when those who worshiped him stopped doing so, or when Ancient Egypt was annexed by the Romans in 30 BC.

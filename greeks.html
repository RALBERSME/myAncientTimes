The Greeks, the Romans and Ancient Egypt


Ancient Greece and ancient Egypt maintained trade relations that can be traced back to the time of the Minoan culture of the Bronze Age. Greeks had been present in Egypt since at least the 7th century BC. In the 7th century BC, after the Greek Dark Age from 1100 to 750 BC, the city of Naukratis was founded on the Nile in ancient Egypt. It was the first and, for much of its early history, the only permanent Greek colony in Egypt and acted as a hub for the exchange of Greek and Egyptian art and culture. The port of Herakleion served as the main seaport for Greco-Egyptian trade. Alexander the Great conquered Egypt in an early phase of his conquests in 331 BC. He respected the pharaonic religions and customs and became pharaoh.

Alexander founded the city of Alexandria on the Mediterranean coast, which was to be the capital of Egypt for centuries. After his death in 323 BC, his empire was divided among the Diadochi. Egypt passed to Ptolemy I Soter, whose descendants founded a Greco-Macedonian royal dynasty of Egypt. Ptolemy gave additional legitimacy to his rule in Egypt by acquiring Alexander's body. He intercepted the embalmed body on its way to burial, brought it to Egypt, and placed it in a golden coffin in Alexandria. This remained one of the city's famous sights for many years until it was probably destroyed in the 3rd century. The Ptolemaic dynasty consisted entirely of ethnic Greeks and produced famous members such as Cleopatra VII. Alexandria became the center of the Greek and Hellenistic world under the Ptolemies and the center of international trade, art and science. The Lighthouse of Alexandria was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, and the Library of Alexandria was the largest library in the world during the reign of Ptolemy II Philadelphus. After the conquest of Egypt by the Roman Empire, Greek influence in Egypt remained large and a Greco-Roman elite ruled over the Egyptian population.

After the division of the empire in 395, Egypt remained under the influence of the Greek-influenced Eastern Roman or Byzantine Empire until the Arab-Islamic conquest in the 7th century. With the rise of the Ottoman Empire in the 15th and 16th centuries, both Greece and Egypt came under Ottoman control. Greek culture and political influence continued during this period and perhaps even reached one of its peaks during the Ottoman rule in Egypt, when many Ottoman governors and pashas of Greek descent ruled Egypt. In 146 BC, Greece fell to the Roman Empire. This ended the political history of independent Greece for almost two millennia.

Conclusion:

The Greeks and Romans adopted a lot from ancient Egypt, they admired the old culture and were interested in its science and technology, but also its mysticism. In the center of St. Peter's Square, for example, there is a 4000-year-old obelisk. This comes from the ancient Egyptian city of Heliopolis and symbolizes the victory over the heretics. It was shipped to Rome by Emperor Caligula and erected in 1586 under Pope Sixtus V. Some people today therefore claim that the Christian religion is also based on the basic ideas of the ancient Egyptians. Whether this is the case is debatable and not proven.
